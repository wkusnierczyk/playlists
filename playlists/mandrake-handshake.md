## Guide
<sub>[↑↑ TOC](#table-of-contents) · [← Playlist](#playlist) · [Songs →](#songs)</sub>

This playlist takes its name from the Brian Jonestown Massacre track that opens it, and that band's sprawling, confrontational, reverb-drenched vision sets the tone for everything that follows. Where The Rainbow playlist mapped a world of silence, space, and emotional restraint, Mandrake Handshake inhabits a different territory entirely: the fuzz-soaked, tambourine-shaking, incense-burning underground where 1960s psychedelia never ended but simply went deeper underground and re-emerged, blinking, in basements and bedrooms across the world.

The Brian Jonestown Massacre dominate the collection with ten tracks drawn from across their enormous catalogue — from the trembling beauty of "Anemone" to the sprawling "Memory Camp." Anton Newcombe's band has always existed in a parallel timeline where the Stones never went disco, the Velvets never broke up, and Spacemen 3 were the biggest band in the world. Their presence here is less that of a single artist and more of a gravitational field, pulling everything else into orbit.

GHOSTWOMAN are the other recurring presence, contributing seven tracks from two albums that together establish a sound of spectral, reverb-drenched minimalism — garage rock played as if through a veil. Magic Shoppe appear five times, their shoegaze-inflected psychedelia providing some of the playlist's most immersive textures. These three acts alone account for nearly half the playlist, creating a dense core of West Coast-influenced psych-rock around which the remaining artists orbit.

The collection's range is wider than it first appears. WITCH's "Home Town" reaches back to 1970s Zambian rock, connecting the playlist to an African psychedelic tradition that developed independently of, yet in parallel with, Western garage rock. Amanaz's "Sunday Morning" deepens this Zamrock thread. Jonathan Wilson's "The Way I Feel" — a Gordon Lightfoot cover from his Laurel Canyon-recorded Gentle Spirit — introduces the singer-songwriter tradition that runs beneath all this fuzz like a hidden aquifer. Father John Misty's "Hollywood Forever Cemetery Sings" brings sardonic wit to the California dreamscape.

A thread of darkness runs through the collection. Psychic Ills' three tracks — "All Alone," "Confusion (I'm Alright)," and "I Don't Mind" — inhabit a zonked, nocturnal space where psych-rock dissolves into something closer to drone. Wooden Shjips' "These Shadows" and The Black Ryder's "Sweet Come Down" occupy similar territory. Holy Wave's "Do You Feel It" and DOPE LEMON's "Honey Bones" push toward a more sun-bleached, lysergic warmth. The tension between these darker and lighter impulses gives the playlist its shape.

What unifies these fifty tracks is less a shared mood than a shared method: the conviction that electric guitars, played through enough reverb and delay, can open doors in the mind. This is music built on drones, on repetition, on the hypnotic power of a riff played until it becomes a mantra. It descends from the Velvet Underground and the 13th Floor Elevators, from Spacemen 3 and the Jesus and Mary Chain, from the Nuggets compilations and the Paisley Underground. But it is not nostalgic — or rather, its nostalgia is for a future that the 1960s promised and never delivered, a world where consciousness expansion was a civic duty and the guitar was the tool of liberation.

### 400
<sub>[↑↑ TOC](#table-of-contents) · [↑ Songs](#songs) · [All Alone →](#all-alone)</sub>

**Artist:** [Colbey](#colbey) · **Album:** [Journey Man](#journey-man)

A slow-burning psych-folk meditation from the otherwise obscure Colbey. The track unfurls at its own unhurried pace, wrapping a simple vocal melody around a bed of warm, fuzzed-out guitar and gently rolling percussion. "400" operates in the space between waking and dreaming, its repetitive structure inducing a trance-like state that aligns it with the zonked, sun-bleached aesthetic running through this playlist. The song's hypnotic quality makes it feel less like a performance and more like an incantation.


### All Alone
<sub>[↑↑ TOC](#table-of-contents) · [↑ Songs](#songs) · [← 400](#400) · [All the Time →](#all-the-time)</sub>

**Artist:** [Psychic Ills](#psychic-ills) · **Album:** [Inner Journey Out](#inner-journey-out)

One of the most narcotic tracks on Psychic Ills' double album Inner Journey Out. "All Alone" drifts on a bed of reverb-soaked guitar and whispered vocals, Tres Warren's voice barely rising above the haze. The song inhabits a nocturnal space where psychedelia dissolves into something closer to ambient drone, the instrumentation stripped back to its barest essentials — a guitar line that circles like smoke, a rhythm that pulses rather than drives. It captures the feeling of a late-night room lit by a single lamp, consciousness flickering at the edges.


### All the Time
<sub>[↑↑ TOC](#table-of-contents) · [↑ Songs](#songs) · [← All Alone](#all-alone) · [All the Way →](#all-the-way)</sub>

**Artist:** [GHOSTWOMAN](#ghostwoman) · **Album:** [Ghost Woman](#ghost-woman)

The opening track from GHOSTWOMAN's self-titled debut, "All the Time" announces the project's aesthetic immediately: gritty tremolo guitar, elastic riffs, and spectral vocal harmonies that sound like they're being transmitted from a distant radio tower. Evan Uschenko's voice floats above the instrumentation in a haze of reverb, never quite anchoring to any one emotional register. The song establishes the album's terrain — garage rock played as if through a veil of distance and memory, equal parts Can and Jefferson Airplane filtered through the dust of a Canadian prairie.


### All the Way
<sub>[↑↑ TOC](#table-of-contents) · [↑ Songs](#songs) · [← All the Time](#all-the-time) · [Along →](#along)</sub>

**Artist:** [Magic Shoppe](#magic-shoppe) · **Album:** [Reverberation](#reverberation)

A track from Magic Shoppe's debut album Reverberation that exemplifies the Boston band's commitment to hypnotic reverb rock. "All the Way" builds on a foundation of metronomic rhythm and cascading guitar feedback, Josiah Webb's vocals buried deep in the mix as the song accumulates layers of drone and shimmer. The track doesn't so much progress as intensify, each pass through its central riff adding new textures of distortion until the whole thing feels like standing inside a cathedral of fuzz.


### Along
<sub>[↑↑ TOC](#table-of-contents) · [↑ Songs](#songs) · [← All the Way](#all-the-way) · [Anemone →](#anemone)</sub>

**Artist:** [GHOSTWOMAN](#ghostwoman) · **Album:** [Ghost Woman](#ghost-woman)

From GHOSTWOMAN's self-titled debut, "Along" finds Evan Uschenko in a more contemplative mode, the guitars pulling back slightly to reveal the song's melodic architecture. There's a Byrds-like quality to the jangle beneath the reverb, a sense of open-road momentum that belies the track's spectral production. The song earned its own music video in 2022, suggesting it held a special place in Uschenko's estimation of the album's offerings.


### Anemone
<sub>[↑↑ TOC](#table-of-contents) · [↑ Songs](#songs) · [← Along](#along) · [Anenome →](#anenome)</sub>

**Artist:** [The Brian Jonestown Massacre](#the-brian-jonestown-massacre) · **Album:** [Their Satanic Majesties' Second Request](#their-satanic-majesties-second-request)

One of the Brian Jonestown Massacre's most beloved songs and arguably Anton Newcombe's finest three minutes. Released on Their Satanic Majesties' Second Request in 1996, "Anemone" is built on a trembling, ascending guitar figure that seems to reach toward something it can never quite touch. The vocal melody is achingly beautiful, Newcombe singing with a rare tenderness that cuts through his usual confrontational persona. The song became the emotional centrepiece of the 2004 documentary DIG!, and has since become the track most people think of when they think of BJM — a shimmering, heartbreaking proof that beneath all the chaos and self-destruction, there was always genuine transcendence.


### Anenome
<sub>[↑↑ TOC](#table-of-contents) · [↑ Songs](#songs) · [← Anemone](#anemone) · [Anne, If →](#anne-if)</sub>

**Artist:** [The Brian Jonestown Massacre](#the-brian-jonestown-massacre) · **Album:** [Tepid Peppermint Wonderland](#tepid-peppermint-wonderland)

A different BJM track from the one at position 16, this "Anenome" (note the alternate spelling) appears on the Tepid Peppermint Wonderland compilation, drawn from the band's sprawling mid-1990s output. Where the more famous "Anemone" is all trembling beauty, this track operates in a darker, more abrasive register — the guitars churn rather than shimmer, and the mood is closer to the aggressive psychedelia of Take It from the Man! than the dreamy pop of Their Satanic Majesties'. The spelling variation appears intentional, distinguishing two different compositions that share a botanical inspiration.


### Anne, If
<sub>[↑↑ TOC](#table-of-contents) · [↑ Songs](#songs) · [← Anenome](#anenome) · [Arline →](#arline)</sub>

**Artist:** [GHOSTWOMAN](#ghostwoman) · **Album:** [Anne, If](#anne-if)

GHOSTWOMAN's sophomore album, released in January 2023 on Full Time Hobby. Arriving quickly on the heels of the self-titled debut, it continues Uschenko's exploration of spectral psych while expanding the sonic palette. One Bandcamp reviewer called it "a psychedelic, multi-genre, mind massager." The band had just finished touring with Dry Cleaning and Chad VanGaalen before release. The album includes "Arline" and the title track "Anne, If."

The title track from GHOSTWOMAN's 2023 sophomore album, "Anne, If" represents an evolution from the debut's lo-fi garage origins toward something more textured and emotionally complex. The song is built on a foundation of psych-grunge distortion but reveals unexpected melodic beauty in its structure, Uschenko's abstract lyrics feeling out the shape of the music rather than conveying literal meaning. One Bandcamp reviewer described the album as a whole as "a psychedelic, multi-genre, mind massager," and this track epitomises that quality.


### Arline
<sub>[↑↑ TOC](#table-of-contents) · [↑ Songs](#songs) · [← Anne, If](#anne-if) · [Behind Your Eyes →](#behind-your-eyes)</sub>

**Artist:** [GHOSTWOMAN](#ghostwoman) · **Album:** [Anne, If](#anne-if)

From GHOSTWOMAN's second album Anne, If, "Arline" continues Evan Uschenko's exploration of spectral, reverb-drenched songcraft. The track moves at a deliberate pace, its guitars alternating between clean arpeggios and waves of distortion, while the vocals maintain their characteristic distance — present but unreachable, like a voice heard through several walls. The song demonstrates the evolution from the self-titled debut's rawer sound toward a more considered, layered approach to production.


### Behind Your Eyes
<sub>[↑↑ TOC](#table-of-contents) · [↑ Songs](#songs) · [← Arline](#arline) · [City Alight (Yeah) →](#city-alight-yeah)</sub>

**Artist:** [GHOSTWOMAN](#ghostwoman) · **Album:** [Ghost Woman](#ghost-woman)

A late-album cut from GHOSTWOMAN's self-titled debut that deepens the record's atmosphere of ghostly intimacy. "Behind Your Eyes" strips the instrumentation back further than most of the album's tracks, allowing the interplay between guitar and voice to carry the emotional weight. The title suggests introspection, an attempt to see through another person's perception, and the music matches this with a quality of patient searching — each note placed with care, each silence given room to breathe.


### City Alight (Yeah)
<sub>[↑↑ TOC](#table-of-contents) · [↑ Songs](#songs) · [← Behind Your Eyes](#behind-your-eyes) · [Clockwork →](#clockwork)</sub>

**Artist:** [Magic Shoppe](#magic-shoppe) · **Album:** [Interstellar Car Crash](#interstellar-car-crash)

The closing track from Magic Shoppe's Interstellar Car Crash EP, "City Alight (Yeah)" was singled out by The Big Takeover as one of their favourite songs of the year, praising its "magnetic" and "stellar" qualities. The song builds from a hypnotic foundation into something genuinely euphoric, the "Yeah" in the title manifesting as a release of tension after the EP's preceding explorations of feedback and drone. It captures that specific moment when a city's lights first appear on the horizon after a long night drive — the promise of arrival after sustained motion.


### Clockwork
<sub>[↑↑ TOC](#table-of-contents) · [↑ Songs](#songs) · [← City Alight (Yeah)](#city-alight-yeah) · [Confusion (I'm Alright) →](#confusion-im-alright)</sub>

**Artist:** [GHOSTWOMAN](#ghostwoman) · **Album:** [Ghost Woman](#ghost-woman)

Another entry from GHOSTWOMAN's self-titled debut, "Clockwork" lives up to its name with a metronomic, mechanistic quality that sets it apart from the album's more organic tracks. The rhythm section locks into a tight, repetitive groove while the guitars weave patterns of fuzz and tremolo above, creating a tension between human performance and machine-like precision. It's the kind of track that rewards extended listening — the apparent simplicity revealing layers of detail on each pass.


### Confusion (I'm Alright)
<sub>[↑↑ TOC](#table-of-contents) · [↑ Songs](#songs) · [← Clockwork](#clockwork) · [Days, Weeks and Moths →](#days-weeks-and-moths)</sub>

**Artist:** [Psychic Ills](#psychic-ills) · **Album:** [Inner Journey Out](#inner-journey-out)

From Psychic Ills' Inner Journey Out, "Confusion (I'm Alright)" inhabits the album's characteristic twilight zone where psych-rock melts into something more ambient and disoriented. The parenthetical reassurance in the title reads as darkly ironic — the music itself suggests anything but certainty. The guitar tone is cool and detached, floating over a rhythm that shuffles rather than drives, while Warren's whispered vocals offer comfort that feels increasingly uncertain. One reviewer noted the track's "cool guitar sound" but suggested it could have been better complemented by more vocal presence — though the restraint is arguably the point.


### Days, Weeks and Moths
<sub>[↑↑ TOC](#table-of-contents) · [↑ Songs](#songs) · [← Confusion (I'm Alright)](#confusion-im-alright) · [Disguise →](#disguise)</sub>

**Artist:** [The Brian Jonestown Massacre](#the-brian-jonestown-massacre) · **Album:** [Revelation](#revelation)

A sprawling track from the Brian Jonestown Massacre's 2014 album Revelation, recorded at Anton Newcombe's Cobra Studios in Berlin. "Days, Weeks and Moths" represents the band's later, more expansive phase, where the tight three-minute pop songs of the 1990s gave way to longer, more meditative explorations. The title itself suggests the passage of time measured in increasingly strange units, and the music matches this with a slow, accumulating grandeur — layers of guitar and drone building toward something that never quite resolves, leaving the listener suspended in a perpetual present.


### Disguise
<sub>[↑↑ TOC](#table-of-contents) · [↑ Songs](#songs) · [← Days, Weeks and Moths](#days-weeks-and-moths) · [Do You →](#do-you)</sub>

**Artist:** [The Spyrals](#the-spyrals) · **Album:** [The Spyrals](#the-spyrals)

From The Spyrals' 2012 self-titled debut, "Disguise" showcases the San Francisco trio's ability to transform straightforward garage rock into something genuinely psychedelic. The second half of the track opens into a lysergic swirl of shimmering guitars and gigantic guitar licks, the song's structure dissolving as the instruments take flight. It's the kind of track where the destination matters less than the journey — the disguise of the title perhaps referring to the way the song presents itself as simple rock before revealing its hallucinatory depths.


### Do You
<sub>[↑↑ TOC](#table-of-contents) · [↑ Songs](#songs) · [← Disguise](#disguise) · [Do You Feel It →](#do-you-feel-it)</sub>

**Artist:** [GHOSTWOMAN](#ghostwoman) · **Album:** [Ghost Woman](#ghost-woman)

A track from GHOSTWOMAN's self-titled debut that asks its central question through texture rather than lyrics. "Do You" rides a wave of tremolo guitar and understated rhythm, Uschenko's vocal delivery maintaining the album's characteristic ambiguity — words that seem to mean something specific but dissolve on closer inspection into pure sound. The song's power lies in its refusal to resolve, the question of its title hanging in the air like the reverb that trails from every chord.


### Do You Feel It
<sub>[↑↑ TOC](#table-of-contents) · [↑ Songs](#songs) · [← Do You](#do-you) · [Forever Certain →](#forever-certain)</sub>

**Artist:** [Holy Wave](#holy-wave) · **Album:** [Relax](#relax)

The opening track from Holy Wave's album Relax, "Do You Feel It" sets the tone for an album of lysergic, sun-drenched garage rock from this El Paso-born, Austin-based quintet. KUTX noted that the song borrows Roky Erickson's elliptical guitar riff from the 13th Floor Elevators' "Don't Fall Down" and grafts it onto a laid-back groove — a conscious tipping of the cap to psych-rock's Texan originators. The track was recorded with producer Erik Wofford at Cacophony Studios in Austin, and its dreamy vocal wash and jangly guitars announce Holy Wave's commitment to the 1960s psychedelic tradition while maintaining a distinctly contemporary slackness.


### Forever Certain
<sub>[↑↑ TOC](#table-of-contents) · [↑ Songs](#songs) · [← Do You Feel It](#do-you-feel-it) · [Fudge →](#fudge)</sub>

**Artist:** [Tracy Bryant](#tracy-bryant) · **Album:** [A Place for Nothing and Everything](#a-place-for-nothing-and-everything)

The standout track from Tracy Bryant's 2017 album A Place for Nothing and Everything in Its Place, "Forever Certain" was described by LA Record as Bryant's "most explicitly catchy song to date," built around a deceptively simple chord progression that lodges itself in the brain. Bryant recorded the album in Germany with producer Leonard Kaage, and "Forever Certain" represents the moment where his lo-fi garage-psych beginnings fully evolve into something more open and melodically confident — the fuzz recedes just enough to let the pop songwriting shine through, a Ray Davies sensibility filtered through California reverb.


### Fudge
<sub>[↑↑ TOC](#table-of-contents) · [↑ Songs](#songs) · [← Forever Certain](#forever-certain) · [Geneva Strangemod →](#geneva-strangemod)</sub>

**Artist:** [The Brian Jonestown Massacre](#the-brian-jonestown-massacre) · **Album:** [The Future Is Your Past](#the-future-is-your-past)

From the Brian Jonestown Massacre's 2017 album The Future Is Your Past, "Fudge" is a characteristic product of Anton Newcombe's Berlin period — recorded at his Cobra Studios and co-produced with Fabien Leseure. The track embodies the band's later approach: less raw confrontation than the 1990s material, more a kind of stately, droning hypnosis where the guitars layer and interlock over a steady motorik pulse. The title's playful simplicity belies the music's seriousness of purpose.


### Geneva Strangemod
<sub>[↑↑ TOC](#table-of-contents) · [↑ Songs](#songs) · [← Fudge](#fudge) · [Golden Hour →](#golden-hour)</sub>

**Artist:** [Glyders](#glyders) · **Album:** [Maria's Hunt](#marias-hunt)

A track from Glyders' acclaimed debut Maria's Hunt that exemplifies the Chicago duo's "maximal minimal" approach. Joshua Condon and Eliza Weber built the album's sound at their home studio they dubbed the Juicy Lagoon, embracing analogue recording and lo-fi aesthetics. "Geneva Strangemod" rides a hypnotic, repetitive riff through its duration, the song's structure simple but its textures rich and layered — somewhere between T. Rex's glam stomp and the motorik pulse of krautrock, all captured with the warm crackle of tape.


### Golden Hour
<sub>[↑↑ TOC](#table-of-contents) · [↑ Songs](#songs) · [← Geneva Strangemod](#geneva-strangemod) · [Gun Shy →](#gun-shy)</sub>

**Artist:** [Glyders](#glyders) · **Album:** [Maria's Hunt](#marias-hunt)

One of the standout tracks from Glyders' Maria's Hunt, described by The Quietus as possessing "THC motorik" — a perfect encapsulation of its buzzy, trance-inducing groove. The song occupies the psych-rock side of the album's dual personality, driving forward with a relentless, circular riff that seems designed to induce an altered state through sheer repetition. Condon's spectral vocals float above the churn, adding a ghostly dimension to what is fundamentally a song about propulsion and momentum. The Dusted review called it "a roadhouse band in the bardo, stinging riffs puncturing misty miasmas of cosmic contemplation."


### Gun Shy

### Home Town
<sub>[↑↑ TOC](#table-of-contents) · [↑ Songs](#songs) · [← Hollywood Forever Cemetery Sings](#hollywood-forever-cemetery-sings) · [Honey Bones →](#honey-bones)</sub>

**Artist:** [WITCH](#witch) · **Album:** [Witch: We Intend to Cause Havoc!](#witch-we-intend-to-cause-havoc)

A track from WITCH's debut album Introduction, originally released in 1972 as the first commercially released record by a Zambian band. "Home Town" finds vocalist Emanuel "Jagari" Chanda singing about longing for home over a churning garage-rock groove that fuses Western psychedelia with Southern African rhythms. WITCH — an acronym for "We Intend To Cause Havoc" — were the most popular band in 1970s Zambia, playing marathon shows that sometimes lasted seven hours. The song connects this playlist to the Zamrock movement, a fusion born from Zambia's post-independence golden days when President Kenneth Kaunda decreed that 95% of music on the radio had to be Zambian, forcing musicians to create something wholly new from their Western influences.

### Amanaz
<sub>[↑↑ TOC](#table-of-contents) · [↑ Artists](#artists) · [The Black Ryder →](#the-black-ryder)</sub>

**Songs in playlist:** [Sunday Morning](#sunday-morning)

Amanaz — an acronym for "Ask Me About Nice Artistes in Zambia" — were a five-piece band from Lusaka who recorded their sole album Africa in 1973 at Malachite Studios in Chingola. Formed from the remnants of several disbanded groups, the band was led by guitarist Isaac Mpofu from the Wrong Number, with vocalist Keith Kabwe from the Clusters, John Kanyepa from the Black Souls, Jerry Mausala and Watson Lungu from MacBeth. Their album was reissued by Now-Again Records in 2010, introducing Zamrock to a global audience. The album title, according to Kabwe, referred to "how [Africa] was shared and how its inhabitants were butchered and enslaved, its resources stolen." Travis Scott sampled their song "Nsunka Lwendo" on his 2023 album Utopia, and "Khala My Friend" has appeared in Ted Lasso, High Maintenance, and The Comedy.


### The Black Ryder

An Australian shoegaze duo formed in 2007 by Aimee Nash and Scott Von Ryper after their exit from The Morning After Girls. Their debut Buy the Ticket, Take the Ride (2009) was praised as one of the leading records in the shoegaze/dream-pop revival, described as "woozy, droning and totally sublime shoegaze with a dark psychedelic slant." Their second album The Door Behind the Door followed in 2015. A box set released by Now-Again Records compiling WITCH's recordings from the 1970s. The set documents the arc of Zambia's most popular band from their garage-rock debut Introduction (1972) through their funk-inflected later work.
